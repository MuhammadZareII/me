About

I’ve always worked in electronics, but not in the narrow sense of just designing circuits. For me, it’s one continuous system that starts from physics and extends all the way to working machines — from the physics of devices, to circuit and hardware design, to embedded systems and higher-level algorithms.

What interests me is the connection between layers. The same electron that follows quantum mechanics in a material ends up shaping a signal on a PCB, a control loop in firmware, or data going into a model. I haven’t really switched fields over time — I’ve just worked at different levels of the same system, trying to understand how these layers fit together and influence one another.

My work reflects that. I’ve built optical and electromagnetic simulations, designed circuits and PCBs, developed embedded systems, and worked on perception and machine learning problems. Many of these ideas have been tested in real systems, from robotics platforms to sensing and communication setups, where constraints and imperfections matter as much as theory. Different tools, same mindset: understand the system from the ground up, and make sure it actually works in practice.

I care about physical correctness. Whether it’s checking energy conservation in an optical model or validating a hardware system on the bench, I don’t like treating simulations or abstractions as black boxes.

This portfolio is organized the same way I think — as a stack. From physics and simulation at the bottom, through hardware and embedded systems, up to higher-level intelligence. Each layer builds on the previous one, and understanding their interaction is a central part of how I work.

Research Interests

Two areas I am actively reading and thinking about.

D2NN & Optical Analog Computing

Diffractive Deep Neural Networks · Angular Spectrum Method · Physical AI

Diffractive Deep Neural Networks (D2NN, Lin et al. 2018) perform inference entirely in the optical domain: phase masks replace weight matrices, and computation happens at the speed of light through free-space diffraction. My interest is in the physical feasibility gap — the distance between a network that converges in simulation and one that can actually be fabricated. This involves sampling theory (ASM band-limit), energy conservation, photon-budget analysis, SNR under detector noise, and Monte Carlo fabrication tolerance analysis. More broadly, I am interested in what it means to implement neural network operations with physical substrates rather than transistors.

Angular Spectrum Method FDTD Optical Computing Physical AI Wave Optics

QISAT — Quantum Integrated Sensing & Communication

CV-QKD · Distributed Optical Fiber Sensing · ISAQN

The Xu et al. 2024 ISAQN framework (Integrated Sensing And Quantum communication Networks) demonstrates that quantum key distribution (CV-QKD) and distributed optical fiber sensing (DOFS) can coexist on the same fiber through frequency-division multiplexing. The sensing channel uses Rayleigh backscattering for vibration sensing, with pilot-tone phase recovery bringing it to the quantum noise limit — meaning the sensing precision is bounded by quantum mechanics, not classical electronics. I am interested in this convergence: using the same infrastructure for both secure communication and physical-world sensing, and the signal processing that makes both work simultaneously without mutual degradation.
